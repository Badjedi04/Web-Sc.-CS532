</div><p>Is the Democratic Party a group of people trying to really help the poor?</p>
<p>Let us take a close look at the Democratic Party to see what it is and what Democrats have hidden to keep the public unaware of their true history, a history that would destroy their voters’ support. We will examine a few ideas shown by others and backed up by documents. Let us look at a brief, but inclusive, background of the Democratic Party. We state “inclusive” because the Democratic Party does not include its entire history because if it did, it fears that many people will wake up and see just who they really are and they will lose their power. Let us now show that the Democrats leave out much of their early history from 1856 to 1876. One may wonder why they would do that. This is because had the Democrats included those times, they would show their support for slavery, among a number of other ideas that today would make it very easy to label them as true racists due to their actions.</p>

<blockquote><p><strong>“</strong><strong>For more than 200 years, our party has led the fight for civil rights</strong><strong>, health care, Social Security, workers’ rights, and women’s rights. <p>While Democrats state they are leaders in “civil rights” and other minority causes, the history of what they did from around 1856 to today indicates otherwise.</p>
<p>Let us show a l<a href="http://archive.discoverthenetworks.org/groupProfile.asp?grpid=6214">ist composed by Jeffery Lord</a> of what the Democrats do not wish to show in their history. Once a normal person reads this, they will know that by today’s Democratic ideology, the Democrats are guilty of what they are actually guilty of doing.</p><div class="code-block code-block-2" style="margin: 8px 0; clear: both;">

<blockquote><p><strong><a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">Jeffrey Lord</a>, a contributing editor to <em>The American Spectator</em> and a former aide to Jack Kemp and Ronald Reagan, has chronicled the following vital facts about the Democratic Party of 1800 through the 1960s:</strong></p></blockquote>
<ul>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong>Seven Democrat presidents <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">owned slaves</a> between 1800 and 1861.</strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong>Between 1840 and 1860, there were six Democratic Party platforms <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">supporting slavery</a>.</strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong>From 1868-1948, there were 20 Democratic Party platforms that either openly supported <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">segregation</a> or were silent on the subject.</strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong><em>The infamously racist “Jim Crow laws” of the post-Civil War era — mandating segregation in virtually all public settings throughout the South — were <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">passed</a> enthusiastically by Democrats.</em></strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong><u>In the post-Civil War era, the Democratic Party and the Ku Klux Klan had a very <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">close relationship</a>. Columbia University historian Eric Foner writes that the Klan became “a military force serving the interests of the Democratic Party.” And according to University of North Carolina historian Allen Trelease, the Klan served as the “terrorist arm of the Democratic Party.”</u></strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong><em>Democrats <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">opposed</a> the 13th, 14th and 15th amendments to the Constitution. The 13th banned slavery. The 14th effectively overturned the 1857 </em></strong><em><strong>Dred Scott</strong></em><strong><em> decision (made by Democratic pro-slavery Supreme Court justices) by guaranteeing due process and equal protection to former slaves. And the 15th gave black Americans the right to vote.</em></strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong>Democrats <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">opposed</a> the Civil Rights Act of 1866, which was passed by a Republican Congress over the veto of President Andrew Johnson, who had been a Democrat before joining Abraham Lincoln’s Republican ticket in 1864. The law gave blacks with the right to own private property, sign contracts, file lawsuits, and serve as witnesses in a legal proceeding.</strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong><em>Democrats <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">opposed</a> the Civil Rights Act of 1875, which was passed by a Republican Congress and signed into law by Republican President Ulysses S. Grant. The law prohibited racial discrimination in public places and public accommodations.</em></strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong><u>The Democratic Party’s <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">1904 platform</a> used the term “Sectional and Racial Agitation” to condemn the Republican Party’s protests against segregation and against the denial of voting rights to blacks. This “agitation,” said the Democratic platform, sought to “revive the dead and hateful race and sectional animosities in any part of our common country,” which in turn would bring “confusion, distraction of business, and the reopening of wounds now happily healed.”</u></strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong>Between 1908 and 1920, there were 4 Democratic platforms that were <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">silent</a> on the issues of blacks, segregation, lynching, and voting rights. By contrast, the Republican platforms of those years specifically addressed “Rights of the Negro” (1908), opposed lynching (in 1912, 1920, 1924, 1928), and (with the advent of the New Deal) the dangers of turning blacks into “wards of the state.”</strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<blockquote><p><strong><u>At the direction of Democratic President Woodrow Wilson in 1913, Democrats <a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB121856786326834083">segregated</a> the federal government.</u></strong></p></blockquote>
</li>
